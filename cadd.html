Computer Aided Drug Design/Discovery

Instructor: Jitesh Doshi

Role of computers have been undeniably important in today’s science. Every stream of science is utilizing the great power of computers in effective research and discovery. In complex field of life sciences, computers do not always help to solve the problem completely, but sure do they provide an alternative experimentation for many processes. This has been possible of course because of years of developments in science, and knowledge and data it has generated so far. Discovering new molecules for curing diseases is definitely not an easy science, but demands a large amount of financial, temporal and highly manual inputs. We definitely are far away from computers alone discovering a new molecule for this purpose, nevertheless, over the years, computers have been helping a great deal in reducing the money, time and efforts which are to be put in this process.

        That’s where this new buzzword - CADD or Computer-Aided or Computer-Assisted Drug Discovery is coming in to the picture. I am definitely not an expert in this field. I come from Bioinformatics background and that kind of gives me a unique perspective towards the computer science as well as life science. When I started taking these basic level workshops in CADD, mostly about the tools available and how to use them. During these workshops, I myself have learnt a lot and it was an amazing experience with young students. But one thing I realised is that in India, students are not exposed to these things, not even basic computing skills required in this digital age, from early on in their academics. For these extremely basic level workshops, I have created some small exercises practice these tools. Here I am collating them together with a hope that this may help others who are using these software tools and with an aim that I keep improving them continuously as time goes so as to include more details, as at this stage, they are complementary to my classroom teachings and not whole on their own. Some of the things may come from some online sources, I’ll try and search for them and cite it wherever necessary. I am also planning to upload small and quick video tutorials for all of these in the coming days, so any suggestions, inputs will be valuable.

Tools used in this course:

A variety of freely available tools, databases and web-servers will be used. If you plan to finish the entire series of tutorials, it’ll be a good idea to first make sure you have all these software installed on your computer. You can find a link to all those software at [Link]

Notepad

A simple text editor

MarvinSketch

Basic molecule editing/building tool and to calculate some basic properties of molecules

Chimera

A structure visualizer, and an extendible molecular modelling system, will be used for protein structure visualization and also as a wrapper to docking using AutoDock Vina and Homology modelling using Modeller

Modeller

A widely used tool for homology modelling

AutoDockTools

Preparation of Molecules for docking using Autodock Vina

AutoDock Vina

Perform Protein-Ligand Docking

SmiLib

Combinatorial Library Generation

We also will be exploring some freely available databases like PubChem, UniProt, Therapeutic Target Databases etc. along with some web-based tools like swissADME, Online Chemical Database and so on.

Drug Discovery Workflow:

Drug discovery, especially Structure Based Drug Discovery process is a very complex process. Here are some of the steps which we can understand from some basic searches and analysis.

Here is a list of tutorials and a final, small project to put all these tools to use into a continuous workflow

  • Exploring PubChem Compound Database
  • Representation of Molecular Structures using various formats in MarvinSketch
  • Using SmiLib to generate combinatorial library
  • Using Chimera as a tool to visualize molecules and molecular interactions
  • Using Chimera’s interface to Modeller for Homology Modelling
  • Using AutoDock Tools and AutoDock Vina to perform docking
  • Using Chimera’s interface to Vina for molecular docking
